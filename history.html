A Brief History of Hypertext & the Web

The story of the World Wide Web begins long before Tim Berners-Lee's famous 1989 proposal. It traces back decades — to visionaries who imagined a new way of organising human knowledge.

Timeline of Key Events

1945

Vannevar Bush — The Memex

In his essay As We May Think, Vannevar Bush described a hypothetical device called the "Memex" — a desk-based machine that could store books, records, and communications, allowing users to follow associative trails through information. This concept foreshadowed hypertext.

1963

Ted Nelson Coins "Hypertext"

American philosopher and technology pioneer Ted Nelson formally coined the term hypertext to describe non-sequential writing — text that branches and allows choices to the reader. He later developed the Xanadu project, an early vision of a global hypertext system.

1968

Douglas Engelbart's "Mother of All Demos"

Engelbart demonstrated a live system with hypertext links, collaborative editing, video conferencing, and a computer mouse — decades ahead of its time. His oN-Line System (NLS) was the first to use linked, structured documents.

1980

Berners-Lee Writes ENQUIRE

While working at CERN, Tim Berners-Lee wrote ENQUIRE — a personal database programme that stored information using cards linked by hypertext-style connections. This was his first practical experiment with linked documents.

1989

The Proposal: "Information Management: A Proposal"

On 12 March 1989, Berners-Lee submitted his landmark proposal to CERN management. His supervisor, Mike Sendall, famously wrote on the cover: "Vague but exciting." The proposal outlined a distributed hypertext system to manage CERN's research data.

1990

First Web Server & Browser

Berners-Lee developed the first web server (httpd) and the first web browser/editor (WorldWideWeb, later renamed Nexus), both running on a NeXT workstation at CERN. The three foundations — HTTP, HTML, and URLs — were established.

1991

The Web Goes Public

The first website — info.cern.ch — went live and was made publicly accessible outside CERN. It described the Web project itself and explained how to set up a web server.

1993

NCSA Mosaic Browser

The Mosaic browser, developed at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications, was the first to display images inline with text. It popularised the Web among non-technical users and triggered rapid commercial growth.

1994

World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) Founded

Berners-Lee founded the W3C at MIT to develop open web standards and ensure the Web remained interoperable and freely accessible — not controlled by any single company.

Today

A Global Hypertext System

Over 5 billion people use the Web daily. What began as a tool for sharing physics research notes has become the world's largest information system, economy, and communication network.

Did You Know?

  • Berners-Lee never patented the Web — he gave it to humanity for free
  • The first photo uploaded to the Web was of a CERN band called Les Horribles Cernettes
  • The "www" prefix is technically optional on most websites
  • The first web server sign read: "This machine is a server. DO NOT POWER IT DOWN!!"

The Role of CERN

CERN — the European Organisation for Nuclear Research — is the world's largest particle physics laboratory, located on the French-Swiss border near Geneva. In the late 1980s, CERN employed thousands of scientists and engineers from over 100 countries, all needing to share research and data.

The challenge was managing a constantly changing set of documents and people. Berners-Lee's insight was that a decentralised hypertext system — where any document could link to any other, without central coordination — could solve this problem at scale.

"I just had to take the hypertext idea and connect it to the TCP and DNS ideas and — ta-da! — the World Wide Web." — Tim Berners-Lee
